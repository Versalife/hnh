Real analysis is the rigorous study of the real number system and functions defined on it.
These notes build up from the completeness axiom to sequences, series, and the Cauchy criterion.

## The Real Numbers

The real numbers $\mathbb{R}$ form the unique (up to isomorphism) complete ordered field.
The word *complete* is doing the essential work:

<TheoremBox type="axiom" title="Completeness Axiom">
Every nonempty subset $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ that is bounded above has a *least upper bound*
(supremum) $\sup S$ in $\mathbb{R}$.
</TheoremBox>

This axiom rules out $\mathbb{Q}$: the set $\{x \in \mathbb{Q} : x^2 < 2\}$ is bounded above
but has no rational supremum. Every theorem in real analysis ultimately traces back to it.

A useful consequence is the **Archimedean property**: for any $x \in \mathbb{R}$ there exists
$n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $n > x$. Equivalently, $\inf\{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\} = 0$.

## Sequences

<TheoremBox type="definition" title="Sequence">
A *sequence* in $\mathbb{R}$ is a function $a : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R}$.
We write $(a_n)_{n=1}^\infty$ or simply $(a_n)$.
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox type="definition" title="Convergence">
$(a_n)$ *converges* to a limit $L \in \mathbb{R}$, written $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = L$, if

$$\forall\, \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists\, N \in \mathbb{N},\ \forall\, n \geq N : |a_n - L| < \varepsilon.$$
</TheoremBox>

The adversary picks an error tolerance $\varepsilon > 0$; we must exhibit a threshold $N$ beyond
which every term lies within $\varepsilon$ of $L$.

<Callout type="note">
Convergence is a tail property. Changing any finite number of terms cannot affect whether
$(a_n)$ converges or what it converges to.
</Callout>

**Example.** $a_n = 1/n \to 0$.

*Proof.* Given $\varepsilon > 0$, the Archimedean property gives $N \in \mathbb{N}$ with $N > 1/\varepsilon$.
Then for all $n \geq N$:

$$|a_n - 0| = \frac{1}{n} \leq \frac{1}{N} < \varepsilon. \qquad \square$$

### Algebra of Limits

<TheoremBox type="theorem" title="Limit Laws">
If $a_n \to L$ and $b_n \to M$, then:
- $a_n + b_n \to L + M$
- $c \cdot a_n \to cL$ for any constant $c \in \mathbb{R}$
- $a_n \cdot b_n \to L \cdot M$
- $a_n / b_n \to L / M$ whenever $M \neq 0$
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox type="theorem" title="Squeeze Theorem">
If $a_n \leq b_n \leq c_n$ for all sufficiently large $n$, and $a_n \to L$ and $c_n \to L$,
then $b_n \to L$.
</TheoremBox>

## Monotone Sequences

<TheoremBox type="theorem" title="Monotone Convergence Theorem">
Every bounded monotone sequence converges. Specifically:
- If $(a_n)$ is increasing and bounded above, then $a_n \to \sup_n a_n$.
- If $(a_n)$ is decreasing and bounded below, then $a_n \to \inf_n a_n$.
</TheoremBox>

This theorem is the completeness axiom in sequence form. It is often the easiest route to
convergence when you can establish both monotonicity and a bound.

## Cauchy Sequences

The Cauchy criterion gives a characterization of convergence that requires no knowledge of
the limit in advance.

<TheoremBox type="definition" title="Cauchy Sequence">
$(a_n)$ is *Cauchy* if

$$\forall\, \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists\, N \in \mathbb{N},\ \forall\, m, n \geq N : |a_m - a_n| < \varepsilon.$$
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox type="theorem" title="Cauchy Criterion">
A sequence of real numbers converges if and only if it is Cauchy.
</TheoremBox>

<Callout type="note">
The forward direction (convergent $\Rightarrow$ Cauchy) holds in any metric space.
The converse (Cauchy $\Rightarrow$ convergent) characterizes *complete* metric spaces.
$\mathbb{R}$ is complete; $\mathbb{Q}$ is not.
</Callout>

## Subsequences

<TheoremBox type="definition" title="Subsequence">
Given $(a_n)$ and a strictly increasing function $k : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$,
the sequence $(a_{k(n)})_{n=1}^\infty$ is a *subsequence* of $(a_n)$.
</TheoremBox>

<TheoremBox type="theorem" title="Bolzano–Weierstrass">
Every bounded sequence in $\mathbb{R}$ has a convergent subsequence.
</TheoremBox>

*Proof sketch.* Suppose $(a_n) \subset [a, b]$. Bisect $[a, b]$; at least one half contains
infinitely many terms — call it $[a_1, b_1]$. Recurse. The nested closed intervals
$[a_1, b_1] \supset [a_2, b_2] \supset \cdots$ with $b_k - a_k = (b-a)/2^k$ pin down a unique
point $L = \sup_k a_k$. Picking one term $a_{n_k} \in [a_k, b_k]$ at each step yields a
subsequence converging to $L$. $\square$

<Callout type="aside">
Bolzano–Weierstrass is compactness of closed bounded intervals in disguise.
The generalization: every sequence in a compact metric space has a convergent subsequence.
</Callout>

## Series

A *series* $\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n$ converges if the sequence of partial sums
$S_N = \sum_{n=1}^N a_n$ converges; its value is $\lim_{N \to \infty} S_N$.

<TheoremBox type="theorem" title="Term Test (Necessary Condition)">
If $\sum a_n$ converges, then $a_n \to 0$.
</TheoremBox>

The converse fails: the harmonic series $\sum 1/n$ diverges even though $1/n \to 0$.

<TheoremBox type="theorem" title="Cauchy Condensation Test">
For a decreasing, nonnegative sequence $(a_n)$, $\sum a_n$ converges if and only if
$\sum_{n=0}^\infty 2^n a_{2^n}$ converges.
</TheoremBox>

**Application: the $p$-series.** $\sum n^{-p}$ converges iff $p > 1$.

Apply condensation: the condensed series is $\sum 2^n \cdot (2^n)^{-p} = \sum 2^{n(1-p)}$,
a geometric series with ratio $2^{1-p}$. This converges iff $2^{1-p} < 1$, i.e.\ $p > 1$.
