A common refrain, routinely wielded to explain the apparent slowdown in pharmaceutical productivity, is that we have plucked all the low-hanging fruit. The logic follows that gathering what remains naturally requires a greater expenditure of time and effort. Setting aside whether "productivity" is even the right lens through which to understand what ails the industry (I have an inherent aversion to this sort of economic framing, though pulling on that thread would take us too far afield), it occurs to me that this conventional metaphor is quite underdeveloped. If we are going to rely on it, we should at least try to flesh it out.

First, the very notion of _fruit_ requires a tree. In wielding this image, most people likely envision the tree of knowledge, treating discoveries as its inherent bounties. Yet the metaphor remains entirely silent on the question of the tree’s origin. It assumes a pre-existing structure from which ideas are merely extracted. A more evocative, if distinctly less romantic, metaphor would involve mining. The analogous claim would be that we've exhausted most of the shallow surface deposits, and are thus forced ever deeper into the bowels of the earth to extract the same value.

Even if we grant this passive stance, accepting that we did not plant this proverbial tree and merely wish to benefit from its fecundity, the axis of high and low-hanging fruit remains fundamentally underdetermined. Height is not absolute; it only makes sense relative to the base upon which we are standing. It is relative to the stature of the one doing the picking, and to the length and sturdiness of the ladder being used. Furthermore, to make any meaningful assessment about a fruit’s reachability, it must actually be visible _to you_. You cannot determine how high above the ground a fruit shrouded in total darkness hangs. So, even within the world of this metaphor, we are actually dealing with a quadrant generated by the intersection of two axes: the `<low, high>` axis of accessibility and the `<visible, invisible>` axis of illumination ([Figure 1](#fig-quadrant)). More aptly, we're dealing with a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^2$ (where we use the notion of _subspace_ quite loosely, for, whatever structure this space has, we can be certain that it's not flat!).

So what? you might ask. The value of this expanded view is that it becomes far more actionable. It tells us precisely how to proceed depending on which quadrant we believe we are operating in. We may need to focus our energies on building newer and better ladders, or we may need to develop more powerful spotlights to illuminate the tree—whether that means pointing our existing lights into entirely new territories, or bringing stronger illumination to those areas currently left in the dim shadows.

But through all of this, we must always remember that we can plant trees. We can cultivate them from seedlings or raise them from seeds. We can graft new branches onto existing trunks. Trees require time to mature and flower; our truest work is to create the conditions from which new ideas can emerge. We do not simply exist to reap.
